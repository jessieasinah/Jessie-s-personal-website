My story

From Accra, through Linyi, to a desk in La Défense.

I was born in Accra on a sunny April day in 1989 — an Aries through and through, full of fire, full of curiosity. Numbers came to me first: a diploma in Accounting, then a master's in Economics with a Marketing minor. But I knew very quickly that I didn't want a life that fit inside one country, one industry, or one way of thinking.

So at 25, I packed up and moved to Linyi, China. For two years I taught English to kids in the morning, took Mandarin lessons in the afternoon, and in the evenings helped small Chinese manufacturers reach buyers across Africa. It was the first time I realised that what I'm actually good at isn't accounting or economics — it's translating intent. Between people. Between languages. Between worlds that, on paper, shouldn't understand each other.

In 2016 I came to France for a second master's in International Business at Montpellier Business School. By 2018 I was inside the engine room of French investment banking — first BNP Paribas CIB, then Société Générale CIB, then back to BNP for BCEF. I went from application support, to business analyst, to project implementation, to PMO on a multi-country Core Banking rollout, working with clients like British Petroleum, Nestlé and DHL. I learned to read a balance sheet, fix my own bugs in SQL, and run a workshop in three languages on the same afternoon.

But here's the honest part — I refuse to be only-one-thing. I cook anything I'm curious about: jollof one night, hand-pulled noodles the next, a slow French stew the day after. I host dinners that go on too long because nobody wants to leave. I'm picking up Korean for the fun of it, with Japanese, Arabic and Russian already lined up. I throw myself at new cuisines, new cities, new people, new ideas — because the world keeps offering, and it would feel rude not to say yes.

Languages have shaped me more than degrees ever did. Each one I've learned handed me a culture, and each culture taught me a new way of listening. Twi and Kasem made me a daughter of Ghana. Mandarin made me patient. French made me precise. Korean is teaching me humility. Spanish is just for joy.

Today I'm in Paris, looking outward. I'm at my best in roles that demand both rigour and warmth — programme delivery in banking, of course, but also helping founders, family businesses and small teams turn an idea into something that runs. Call me a project manager, a connector, a translator of intent, a jack of many trades. Whatever the label, the engine underneath is the same: joie de vivre, and the deep conviction that life is meant to be tasted, not endured.
